Parallel Gearbox Design & Assembly

Designed and fabricated a compact two-stage parallel-axis gearbox using SolidWorks and 3D printing. The system uses two 4:1 spur-gear stages to achieve a total 16:1 speed reduction while keeping the frame simple, modular, and easy to assemble.

Project Overview

Compact gearbox built around simplicity, modularity, and manufacturability.

This project focused on designing a small 3D-printable gearbox that could transfer motion through parallel axes while reducing output speed. The design uses a single-piece frame, two large gears, two small gears, one large shaft, and two small shafts.

The main engineering goal was to reduce print and assembly risk. Instead of overcomplicating the housing with dovetails, pins, or multi-part supports, the gearbox frame was designed as a single rigid structure with pre-made shaft holes and open top/bottom sections to reduce print time.

Design Rationale

Why this gearbox architecture?

  • Selected spur gears because they are simple, efficient for parallel-axis power transmission, and easier to 3D print than helical or planetary gears.
  • Used two identical 4:1 reduction stages to reach a 16:1 total ratio while keeping the design understandable and replaceable.
  • Designed a single-piece frame for stronger structure and fewer assembly interfaces.
  • Used hexagonal shaft sections where gears attach to reduce slipping under torque.
  • Kept circular shaft sections at housing contact areas to support smoother rotation.

Assembly Logic

Designed to be easy to assemble and repair.

The assembly sequence was documented using annotated CAD views. The large and small gears are placed in paired compartments, the large shaft is inserted through the first gear pair, and small shafts are installed from each side to complete the second stage.

Modular shafts and sleeves were emphasized because 3D-printed shaft components are more likely to wear or break during assembly and operation. Keeping these parts replaceable makes the design easier to maintain.

Engineering Trade-Offs

Practical decisions for 3D printing.

  • Spur gears over complex gear types: simpler geometry reduced modeling and printing risk.
  • Single-piece frame: improved rigidity but made full-frame replacement necessary if the housing failed.
  • Open housing: reduced print time and made gear placement easier, while still supporting shaft alignment.
  • Replaceable internals: gears, shafts, and sleeves could be reprinted independently if damaged.
